Discover Batanes

Batanes is the smallest and northernmost province of the Philippines, located in the Cagayan Valley region. This breathtaking archipelago consists of 10 islands, only three of which are inhabited: Batan, Itbayat, and Sabtang.

Known for its rolling hills, dramatic cliffs, and traditional stone houses, Batanes offers visitors a unique experience of pristine nature and rich Ivatan culture. The province is often called "The Batanes Islands" and is famous for its stunning landscapes that resemble Scotland or New Zealand.

The Ivatan people, known for their hospitality and resilience, have preserved their unique culture and traditions despite the challenges of typhoons and isolation. Their traditional stone houses (called "cogon houses") are perfectly adapted to the harsh weather conditions of the province.

Must-Visit Attractions

Rolling Hills

Experience the iconic green hills that stretch as far as the eye can see. Perfect for sunrise and sunset viewing with panoramic views of the ocean.

Basco Lighthouse

Climb to the top of this iconic lighthouse for breathtaking 360-degree views of Batan Island and the West Philippine Sea.

Traditional Stone Houses

Admire the unique Ivatan architecture featuring stone walls and cogon roofs, designed to withstand strong winds and typhoons.

Mount Iraya

Challenge yourself with a hike to the summit of this active volcano, the highest point in Batanes at 1,009 meters above sea level.

Chavayan Village

Step back in time at this preserved Ivatan village in Sabtang, showcasing the authentic culture and lifestyle of the indigenous people.

Dramatic Coastlines

Marvel at the rugged cliffs and pristine beaches where the Pacific Ocean meets the West Philippine Sea.

Culture & Heritage

A glimpse into the Ivatan way of life — their architecture, attire, traditions, and time-honored crafts — shaped by the islands’ rugged environment and a deep sense of community.

Farming

Root crops like yam, gabi, and sweet potato thrive in Batanes throughout most of the year. Farmers build stone walls and terraces to shield plots from strong amihan winds and soil erosion. Planting and harvesting follow the seasons and are planned with the whole barrio in mind. Neighbors commonly share tools, exchange labor, and coordinate schedules so fields are tended efficiently. Livestock and small kitchen gardens supplement the staple crops to ensure food security. Knowledge is passed from elders to the young during workdays in the fields. These practices create a resilient food system perfectly adapted to an island environment.

Fishing

Traditional boats head out when seas and winds are favorable, following routes known to generations of fishers. Crews prepare gear the night before and consult the weather with caution and respect. Before a trip, families offer short prayers for safe travel and a good catch. Out at sea, teamwork is essential as fishers read currents and shift techniques as conditions change. Catches are shared fairly and often set aside for neighbors who need them. What cannot be eaten fresh is preserved simply by drying or salting. The rhythm of fishing ties the community closely to the sea.

Ivatan Cuisine

Ivatan food is fresh, hearty, and prepared with minimal seasoning. Common dishes showcase local produce such as uved made from banana pith and vunes from dried gabi stalks. Fresh fish is grilled or stewed and paired with root crops rather than heavy sauces. Turmeric rice adds color and a gentle warmth to daily meals. Portions are sensible and cooking methods favor boiling, grilling, and drying. Families gather around simple spreads that highlight what the islands provide. The cuisine is a reflection of thrift, health, and seasonality.

Rituals and Practices

Rituals mark the turning points of farming and fishing seasons. Short blessings are offered before planting or launching boats to acknowledge nature’s power. Community novenas and shared meals reinforce bonds among families. During fiestas, songs and stories retell local history and the virtues admired by the elders. Respect for elders and visitors is woven into every gathering. These practices are simple but deeply felt and help the community face storms together. Through ritual, memory and identity are kept alive.

Traditional Ivatan Houses

Traditional Ivatan houses use thick stone‑and‑lime walls to resist high winds. Roofs are layered with tightly bound cogon that sheds rain and dries quickly after a storm. Windows are small and oriented to reduce exposure to gales. Materials are gathered locally and construction is often a cooperative effort among neighbors. Many homes stand for decades with only routine repairs. The design is practical yet beautiful in its honesty and durability. These dwellings are the most visible symbol of Ivatan ingenuity.

Traditional Handicrafts

Handicrafts turn local fibers into objects used every day. Nito and buri are woven into strong baskets and market bags. Vakul fibers protect farmers from sun and rain and are crafted with patience and skill. Designs are simple, functional, and long‑lasting. Craftwork gives families supplemental income during the off‑season. Skills are taught at home and in community workshops to keep traditions alive. Buying local crafts helps sustain livelihoods and cultural identity.

Traditional Games

Traditional games bring people together during fiestas and school breaks. Children and adults join teams and cheer each other on with good‑natured competition. Many games use simple equipment that can be made from materials at hand. Friendly rivalries travel from one barangay to another and spark new friendships. Games often end with shared snacks or a community meal. The emphasis is on cooperation and fun rather than prizes. Through play, the Ivatan spirit of hospitality shines.
